1-Page Overview

The Business

"Steven's Charter Flight School" is a premier, hybrid aviation company. It operates as both an FAA-approved pilot training academy (the "School") and an on-demand private air carrier (the "Charter").

The Mission

To provide the highest standard of aviation safety, education, and convenience. We aim to be the region's top-choice provider for aspiring pilots seeking a career and for business clients seeking efficient, reliable travel.

Target Audiences

Flight School:

  • Career-Track Students: Individuals seeking a fast, structured path to becoming a professional pilot.
  • Hobbyist Flyers: Professionals, business owners, or retirees who want to learn to fly for personal transportation or recreation.
  • Existing Pilots: Current pilots seeking advanced ratings, such as an Instrument Rating or Multi-Engine Rating.

Charter:

  • Business Executives: Regional business leaders who need to visit multiple sites, clients, or regional offices in a single day.
  • Time-Sensitive Travelers: Individuals needing to reach destinations not well-served by major airlines or who value privacy.

Unique Selling Proposition (USP)

We are a full-lifecycle aviation partner.

Our hybrid model creates a unique synergy: We offer a seamless pathway from a student's first "Discovery Flight" to a professional pilot career. Our charter clients benefit from a fleet and professional pilot corps that are maintained to the exacting standards of both Part 135 and Part 141 regulations.

Phase 2: FAA Certification & Compliance

Note: These two certification processes (Part 141 & Part 135) are separate, complex, and should be started concurrently. Begin by contacting your local FAA FSDO.

For the Flight School (Part 141)

Schedule Part 141 pre-application meeting
  • Call your local FSDO. State your intent to seek Part 141 certification. This meeting is to establish a relationship and understand their specific requirements.
Submit formal Letter of Intent (Part 141)
  • A formal letter to the FSDO outlining your proposed school, courses, aircraft, and key personnel. This officially starts the process.
Submit formal application (FAA Form 8420-8)
  • This is the official "Application for Pilot School Certification." It must be submitted with your TCOs, syllabus, and other required documents.
Develop & submit Training Course Outlines (TCOs)
  • This is the most time-consuming part. Each course (Private, Instrument, Commercial) needs its own TCO, detailing every lesson, objective, and standard.
Pass FAA inspection (Part 141)
  • The FAA will inspect your facilities, aircraft (logs, ADs), instructor records, and your record-keeping system to ensure they match your manuals.
Receive Part 141 Air Agency Certificate
  • After all phases are complete and any discrepancies are fixed, the FSDO will issue your certificate.

For the Charter Service (Part 135)

Schedule Part 135 pre-application meeting
  • A separate, more intensive meeting with the FSDO. You will need to present your business case and proposed management team.
Submit Pre-application Statement of Intent (PASI)
  • Use FAA Form 8400-6. This formally notifies the FAA and allows them to assign a Certification Project Manager (CPM) to your project.
Submit formal Part 135 application & SOE
  • Includes the Schedule of Events (SOE) - a timeline you and the FAA agree on for submitting all required documents.
Write, submit, & get approval for SOC
  • Statement of Compliance (SOC): A line-by-line document listing every Part 135 rule and *how* your operation will comply (with manual references).
Write, submit, & get approval for GOM
  • General Operations Manual (GOM): Your company's "bible" detailing all flight/pilot procedures. Must be approved by the FAA.
Write, submit, & get approval for GMM
  • General Maintenance Manual (GMM): Details your maintenance procedures, how you track aircraft time, and the Director of Maintenance's duties.
Pass FAA inspection & proving flights (Part 135)
  • FAA inspects aircraft, manuals, and key personnel.
  • Culminates in "proving flights" where FAA inspectors observe your team conduct simulated charter flights.
Receive Part 135 Air Carrier Certificate
  • Once all 5 phases are complete, the FAA issues your certificate and OpSpecs. You are now a legal air carrier.

Phase 3: Asset & Team Setup

Acquire or lease training aircraft (e.g., C172)
  • Ensure aircraft are on a 100-hour inspection schedule.
  • Organize all maintenance records (logs, AD compliance) for FAA inspection.
Acquire or lease charter aircraft
  • Must have a 6-month "exclusive use" lease, at minimum.
  • Aircraft must be formally added to your Part 135 Operations Specifications (OpSpecs).
Secure comprehensive aviation insurance
  • Get hull and liability coverage. Part 135 requires "commercial" liability, which is a higher limit.
  • Your insurer will need to approve your pilots and training programs.
Formally hire Part 141 Chief Flight Instructor
  • Must meet experience requirements of 14 CFR ยง 141.35.
  • Their resume and qualifications must be submitted to the FSDO for approval.
Formally hire Part 135 Management
  • Hire Director of Operations, Chief Pilot, and Director of Maintenance.
  • All three must meet strict 14 CFR ยง 119 experience requirements and be approved *by name* by the FAA.
Hire initial CFIs and Charter Pilots
  • CFIs will conduct Part 61/141 training.
  • Charter pilots must be trained and "check-ridden" according to your Part 135 GOM and added to your roster.

This is a simplified guide to the essential FAA regulations. You must read the full text and consult with your FSDO and aviation legal counsel.

What It Is: These are the base rules for all pilots. Part 61 defines the minimum aeronautical experience (flight hours) and knowledge required to earn a pilot certificate (Private, Commercial, ATP, etc.).

How You'll Use It: Any Certified Flight Instructor (CFI) can train a student under Part 61 rules. It's flexible, "pay-as-you-go," and doesn't require a structured syllabus. Your school can offer Part 61 training from day one to generate revenue while pursuing Part 141 certification.

Source: 14 CFR Part 61 →

What It Is: These are the rules for FAA-certificated pilot schools. To be a Part 141 school, you must have an FAA-approved, structured training curriculum, syllabus, facilities, and aircraft, and you are subject to regular FAA surveillance.

How You'll Use It: This is your "university" track. The main benefit is that your students can be certified with fewer minimum flight hours (e.g., 190 hours for a Commercial Pilot Certificate vs. 250 under Part 61). It's also required for your school to accept G.I. Billยฎ / V.A. benefits.

Source: 14 CFR Part 141 →

What It Is: This is the Air Carrier Certificate that allows you to fly passengers for compensation in an "on-demand" (unscheduled) setting. These rules govern everything from pilot rest times and training to aircraft maintenance and operational procedures.

How You'll Use It: This is your entire charter business. It is a rigorous, airline-style certification that gives clients (and your insurer) confidence in your high standard of safety and professionalism. You cannot legally fly a single charter client for money without this certificate.

Source: 14 CFR Part 135 →
